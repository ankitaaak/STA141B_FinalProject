# STA141B_FinalProject


This research study investigates how different socioeconomic factors relate to crime rates across all the counties in California. To be specific, we are examining whether variables such as unemployment rates, education levels, and median income have an effect on the amount of crime incidences. The socioeconomic data for counties in California were obtained from the United States Department of Economic Research Service. This department complies and organizes census data across the US. The second part of the data which is crime incidence per county was sourced from the Federal Bureau of Investigation’s National Incident-Based Reporting System (NIBRS). To form a large dataset, the two datasets were combined to allow analysis at the county level. This enabled a more general understanding of the regional patterns in crime instead of simple individual city comparisons. After statistical and exploratory analyses, the results show that counties with higher unemployment, lower educational attainment, and lower median income tend to have higher crime rates. It is important to note that the strength of these relationships varies across the different variables. Overall, our analysis provides evidence that socioeconomic conditions are meaningfully associated with crime incidence across counties in California and demonstrates the importance of looking at broader economic and educational factors.
